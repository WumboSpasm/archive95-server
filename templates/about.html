About Archive95

Archive95 is an independent web archive focused on material that predates or has otherwise evaded more mainstream archives.

Background

We are extremely lucky to have the Wayback Machine. Since 1996 they have been working tirelessly to preserve as much of the constantly-evolving web as possible, and for what it's worth, they've done a phenomenal job at it.

However...

  1. The Wayback Machine is not perfect. That's okay, nobody is perfect.
  2. The web is several years older than the Wayback Machine.

#2 is especially unfortunate. That is a good chunk of the web's formative years missing from the foremost web archive. As far as most people are concerned, the web's history starts in the era of Internet Explorer, GeoCities, browser plug-ins and animated GIFs.

What Archive95 Does

Archive95 turns to non-traditional data sources to provide a best-effort reconstruction of the early world wide web. These sources include internet CDs - a long-forgotten genre of CD-ROMs meant to provide an offline approximation of the internet for those who were not yet connected - and data troves found in obscure corners of the web. The material is thoroughly audited for any apparent modifications (ie. changing the URLs of links/images or inserting information into pages, both commonly practiced by internet CDs), which are reverted where possible through an automated, reproducible process. The end result is usually not a perfect restoration (it depends on the source), but it is nonetheless an improvement over what was there before.

Archive95 is not hugely concerned with low-hanging fruit, or material that is already thoroughly preserved and easily accessible. For example, CERN's W3 project website from 1992 and the Stanford Web Archive Portal are well-known and in no danger of disappearing, so do not expect to see their historic material added to Archive95. What you can expect to see, however, is rare captures of the early web that are difficult to track down on their own or are otherwise in desperate need of mirrors.

For example, Einblicke ins Internet - arguably the most comprehensive snapshot of the web in 1995 - was virtually non-existent online aside from an out-of-stock Amazon listing and a brief mention of its inclusion in the Museum of the Moving Image's DVD Dead Drop installation. It is preserved only because one of its co-authors quietly uploaded its contents to an open directory on his university faculty page in 2015, which went entirely unnoticed even by most crawlers (including the Wayback Machine's) until 2020.

Archive95 exists to do these kinds of collections justice.

Source Code

Archive95's website is open-source software licensed under the MIT License; its source code can be found here. Furthermore, the entire dataset is freely available to download on the Internet Archive, so it is possible for anyone to run their own local copy of Archive95.
